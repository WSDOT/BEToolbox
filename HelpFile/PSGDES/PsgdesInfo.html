PRESTRESSED GIRDER DESIGN PROGRAM

DESCRIPTION:

PSGDES is a program for the analysis and design of prestressed girders. Both composite and noncomposite girders may be designed by PSGDES. Washington State standard girder properties are coded in the program for easy input.

PRESTRESSED GIRDER DESIGN:

The Prestressed Girder Design portion of the PSGDES program selects the number and arrangement of harped and straight strands for simple span bridges and prestressed girder bridges made continuous for live loads. If the number of harped and straight strands are given, the program performs a check. Girders are designed as simple spans in flexure for all girders. See the "THEORY" section below for shear design assumptions.

THEORY:

In general, the program follows AASHTO Standard Specifications for Highway Bridges dated 1989. The procedure in the program follows that which would be used by a designer in reaching the same objective.

Dead loads, live loads, and section properties are computed and used to find girder stresses at bottom midspan. In noncomposite girders all loads are applied to the girder. In composite girders, the girder, slab, and diaphragm loads are applied to the girder without slab, while live loads and continuous dead loads (curb and asphalt) are applied to the composite girder with slab.

The composite girder I is based upon the girder section and the transformed slab.

Assuming a value for eccentricity, the required number of strands at midspan are estimated and reduced by one. This ensures that more strands than are needed are not used. Only an even number of straight strands are allowed for standard WSDOT girders and bulb-tees. For simple span girders the program initially harps strands only if the estimated number of strands required exceeds the maximum allowable number of straight strands.

Strands are added if stresses at midspan are above allowable limits or the ultimate capacity of the girder is less than Mu or 1.2Mcr. If stresses at the end of the girder are above allowable limits, more strands are harped. If tension at the top of the girder at harping point is above allowable limits, the harped strands are raised through the center section of the girder.

After all minimum criteria have been met, the program tries to reduce stresses at the end of noncomposite girders and composite girders made continuous for live load. More straight strands are harped until any of the following is true:

  1. Stresses are compressive on the top of noncomposite girders at D/2.
  2. A change would require more strands.
  3. The maximum number of harped strands already exists.
  4. There are only four straight strands left.
  5. A change would leave more harped strands than straight strands.
  6. The hold down force at the harping point would exceed 45 kips.
If the girder is being checked, none of these operations will take place.

Office practice is to design prestressed girders as simple span, even if the girders are made continuous for live load. All moments and shears in the program assume simple span loading.

Shear capacity is based on AASHTO 9.20. The girder web is designed to carry all shears. Mmax and Vi are due to LL+I and added dead load (curb and asphalt) only. Mcr is the cracking moment at the bottom of the girder.

Girders made continuous for live load use a different approach at d/2, d or the end of taper. Vi/Mmax cannot be calculated without a frame analysis. If this value is not given, then the following is assumed:

                Lspan Vi                                                               Lspan Vi
                ----------- = 2 when Lspan <= 65 feet                  ---------- = 5 when Lspan > 65 feet
                Mmax                                                                   Mmax

The girder depth in the AASHTO shear equations is assumed to be:

d = Girder Height + "A" dimension - 4.5" for composite girders.

d = .8 Girder Height for noncomposite girders.

However, Vu is still calculated assuming simple span. Shear calculations at the harping point assume simple span conditions.

CONSTANT VALUES USED:

Ultimate strength of slab concrete - 5.0 kips per square inch. This value is used only to transform the slab. Positions of strands are as for Washington State Standard Girder, filling the lowest positions first. Harped strands are assumed to be bundled between harping points with a C.G. Height of 3 inches above the bottom of the girder unless modified. The top pair of harped strands is normally 2 inches below the top, except for short girders where the rise in strands in normally 2 inches below the top, except for short girders where the rise in strands is reduced just enough to keep the slope of the centroid of the harped strands less than 1 in 7. That way all of the strands are within 1 in 6.

Section properties of Washington State Standard Girder Types W42G, W50G, W58G, and W74G are stored in the program. Standard W35DG and W53DG Deck Bulb Tee girders are also stored in the program.

Standard curb load is a New Jersey barrier. If a New Jersey barrier is used, the curb weight is .427 kips per foot. The curb is normally distributed over three girders. The asphalt weight is added to the curb weight and is based on .15 kips per cubic foot and the girder spacing. If asphalt is present, the New Jersey barrier is assumed to weight .470 kips per foot.

Slab dead load includes the weight of standard intermediate diaphragms, two for spans over 80 feet, one for spans over 40 feet to 80 feet, and none for 40 feet and below. For composite girders, a slab pad is included over the top girder flange width with a depth equal to the difference between "A" and the slab thickness. All concrete weights are taken as 0.16 kip per cubic foot. End diaphragms are not included in the slab weight or end reactions.

Modulus of elasticity of steel = 28,000,000 #/sq. in.

Modulus of elasticity of concrete = W(3/2) x (33) x (f'c)(1/2) with W = 155 or E = 63,700 (f'c)(1/2)

The harping point distance is taken as 0.4 x span length for girder types W58G and W73G and all noncomposite girders, 0.333 x span length for other types. (For nonstandard composite girders, the harping point distance, maximum number of straight strands, and maximum number of harped strands will default to the same values as an equivalent depth standard girder.) Harped strands are bundled in the middle portion of the span. Spacing at girder ends are per standard girder plans. C.G. Bundled strands are 3 inches above bottom of girder at centerline of span for 12 strands or less. For more than 12 strands, the excess is carried in a second bundle at 6 inches from bottom of girder.

The weight of the end block when specified is ignored for flexure design in the program. Design stresses and shear calculations at D/2 include end block properties when end blocks are specified. Reactions calculated by the program include end block weight to the Centerline.

where Æ is the total amount of additional deflections due to creep. This value is often taken as 2.0. The maximum value for t is 720 days.

Initial cambers due to prestressing and girder weight are computed as for composite girders. Curb and asphalt weights are also applied to the noncomposite girder. PICK POINT ANALYSIS

DESCRIPTION:

The pick-point analysis portion of the PSGDES program computes the maximum distance from center line bearing that a girder can be picked up or supported based on stresses at the harping point. Both lifting at release and shipping conditions are examined. Girder stresses at the harping point (point on the girder assumed to be critical during handling) are given for maximum pick-points and any pick-point specified.

Note: The program does not check stresses at the pick point. For large pick point values, the cantilever moment stress combined with the prestressing stress can be critical at the pick point. It is the designer’s responsibility to check this stress.

The program can no longer be run as a separate program, it can only be run as an integrated part of the prestressed girder design portion as described in the first section.  The output, as described below, will be appended to the output for the main PSGDES program.

THEORY:

The program solves a quadratic equation for the pick-point that produces the maximum allowable stress at the harping point. It solves this quadratic for allowable compression, the allowable tension without mild reinforcement, and the allowable tension with mild reinforcement for the lifting case and the shipping case.

These pick-points are compared and the least maximum is reported in the output along with the stresses at the harping point. Stresses at the harping point for the specified pick-point are also specified. The maximum pick-point will not exceed the harping point or midspan.

OUTPUT:

The upper part of the output consists of the heading and an echo of the input or default values used by the program.

PICK-POINT DISTANCES (FROM CL BRG) FOR: -

The least maximum pick-point and the controlling condition (lifting or shipping) are given for each allowable stress.

STRESSES AT HARPING POINT (KSI): - From the above maximum pick-points, the least maximum pick-point is given for the two allowable tensions and stresses at the harping point are given. These stresses can be used to check the mild steel required if the tension exceeds 3(f’c)1/2 or 200 psi.

The program output then reminds the Engineer that the standard specs specify some minimum pick-points. The girder should be designed so that the maximum pick-point is above these minimums.
