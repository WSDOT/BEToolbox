GPile Information

Stiffness Analysis of a Pile Group

GPILE is a program that uses the stiffness method to calculate individual pile loads in a pile group.  The program also condenses the stiffness of the individual piles into a 6x6 stiffness matrix that can be used in STRUDL and SEISAB models.
 

Design Methodology:


This method is only as good as the stiffness springs given for the piles.  Generally, unless the deflections are very minute, pile group response to lateral loads is not elastic.  Enough iterations in the design process should be used to assure that the response springs match the loads.

The first step in the analysis is to estimate the stiffness of the individual piles. Vertical and torsional spring constants can be estimated by the methods outlined in section 4.4.3C of the WSDOT Bridge Design Manual. The LPile+ computer program from Ensoft Inc, is used to find the P/d, M/q, P/q, and M/d spring constants for the two lateral axes of the pile. These spring constants vary with the axial pile load as well as lateral forces and moments. Close results can often be found by using a range of forces and moments to identify a linear range.

The lateral force over lateral displacement (P/d) is found by applying a range of lateral forces to the top of the pile.  Case 2 of LPILE+ is used to hold the pile slope constant at 0.0 at the top.  Use a range of axial loads to verify that P/d is not seriously affected by changes in axial load.  Use a range of lateral loads from very small to higher than the expected required capacity.  The lateral force can then be divided by the deflection at the pile head reported by LPILE+.  Plot the P/d vs. deflection.  A linear range should be apparent from the graph, however some shaft problems did not show any linearity.  For the first try, use the linear value for the spring constant.  Similarly, the M/d can be found from the same runs.

The M/q is found by using Case 4 of LPILE+.  The pile top is held to 0.0 dflection and a range of moments are applies.  The pile top rotation (q) is found by dividing the deflection at the first interval by the interval length.  M/q and P/q are found by dividing the applied moment and computed shear at the pile head by the pile top rotation.  As with the other spring constants, there should be a linear range.

After the pile springs have been estimated for all the piles, they are input along with the pile group geometry and pile group loads.  GPILE then computes the group stiffness matrix for the pile group by changing to global coordinates and summing up the individual pile stiffness matrices.

The pile group matrix is inverted and multiplied by the force matrix to solve for the foundational deflections.  The pile cap is assumed to be rigid and does not deform.  The foundational displacements are changed to pile coordinates (u, v, and w) and the individual pile forces are computed.

If pile deflections are outside the linear range, the spring constants must be adjusted for the pile deflection and the problem rerun.  Iterate until satisfied with the results.

As a final check, LPILE+ can be run with the individual pile loads from GPILE.  The pile deflections reported by both programs should be similar.
 

Theory:

The program and theory are described in "Foundational Analysis and Design" by Bowles, 3rd ed., Chapter 18-9.  This reference should always be read by the first time user.  The program has been changed to better reflect Bridge Office practice.
